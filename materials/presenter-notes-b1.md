# Presenter notes - Practitioner session 1: Classify a system (the aiact-live classifier)

**Session goal (one line).** Practitioners can run the tiering decision in order - prohibited, high-risk, limited, minimal - and classify their real systems using the live classifier, including the Article 6(3) downgrade and its profiling limit.

## Run of show (45 min)
- **0-3 - Welcome.** Classification gates everything downstream; get the tier wrong and every later obligation is wrong.
- **3-18 - Concepts (Part 1 + Part 2).**
  - Part 1 (approx 8 min): first match wins - check prohibited (Art 5) first, then high-risk (Annex III), then limited, else minimal.
  - Part 2 (approx 7 min): the two nuances - the Article 6(3) downgrade, and the profiling trap that overrides it.
- **18-40 - Build-along "Classify your real systems" (approx 22 min).** Learners drive the .aiactbox classifier: pick what a system does, read the tier + obligations + article/annex, then classify their own inventory from a1.
- **40-45 - Q&A.** Reinforce the honesty note: the classifier teaches the reflex, real calls turn on specific facts.

## Preflight
- Open the session page, scroll to the .aiactbox, and change the dropdown to confirm the tier + obligations + basis render for at least two scenarios.
- "Expand all" and confirm the decision-logic SVG renders on the projector.
- Have each learner's a1 scope list ready - the build-along classifies those systems.
- Read the "Honesty note" so you can set expectations: it models Articles 5, 6, 50, and Chapter V, not a legal opinion.

## Never-cut beats
- **The Article 6(3) downgrade AND its limit.** A narrow or preparatory Annex III system can sometimes drop out of high-risk - UNLESS it profiles natural persons. Say it plainly: "profiling is always high-risk, no downgrade."
- **First match wins.** Prohibited is checked first; if a use matches Article 5 it cannot ship and no lower tier is relevant.
- **The classifier is a reflex-builder.** The usual answer for a scenario, not a substitute for checking real facts against the Regulation.

## Cuts if running long
- Have learners classify two systems in the build-along instead of their whole inventory.
- Demo the downgrade nuance yourself rather than having everyone reproduce it in the classifier.

## Quiz answers
1. **B** - Whether it is a prohibited practice (Article 5): first match wins, and prohibited is checked first.
2. **C** - Profiles natural persons: any system that scores, ranks, or predicts about a person is always high-risk, no downgrade.
3. **A** - High-risk (Annex III(4), employment): ranking candidates is profiling, so the Article 6(3) downgrade does not apply.

## Common questions + crisp answers
- "Our tool just assists a human - can we downgrade it?" Only if it does not profile people; profiling blocks Article 6(3) outright.
- "Does the classifier give a legal classification?" No - it gives the usual structural answer; confirm real calls against the Regulation and advice.
- "What if a system matches two tiers?" First match wins in order: prohibited, then high-risk, then limited, then minimal.

## Teaching tips
- Let the classifier do the teaching. Have learners predict the tier before they pick the dropdown option, then check - the surprise is the lesson.
- Run at least one prohibited scenario through it so they see the "no compliance path" outcome, not just high-risk.
- The profiling trap is the moment to slow down. Demo an Annex III system that looks downgradeable, then show why profiling keeps it high-risk.
- Keep saying "first match wins" as they classify; the ordering is what makes the reflex reliable under pressure.
- Reinforce the honesty note at the end so nobody treats a classifier output as a legal opinion - it builds the reflex, the Regulation settles the call.
