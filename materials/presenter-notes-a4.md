# Presenter notes - Leader session 4: Penalties + enforcement

**Session goal (one line).** Leaders can state the three fine tiers, compute their own ceilings, and name who enforces the Act - so governance has a board-level number attached.

## Run of show (45 min)
- **0-3 - Welcome.** Why the number matters to a board: fines here are a percentage of global turnover, so they scale with the whole company, not the EU slice.
- **3-20 - Concepts (Part 1 + Part 2).**
  - Part 1 (approx 9 min): Article 99's three fine tiers - 7%/EUR 35M for prohibited practices, 3%/EUR 15M for most obligation breaches, 1%/EUR 7.5M for supplying incorrect information.
  - Part 2 (approx 8 min): who enforces (Articles 64, 74, 101) - the AI Office for GPAI, national market surveillance authorities for high-risk systems.
- **20-40 - Exercises.**
  - Exercise 1 "Compute your ceilings" (approx 12 min): learners calculate each tier against their own turnover.
  - Exercise 2 "Rank what authorities weigh" (approx 8 min): what mitigates or aggravates a fine.
- **40-45 - Q&A.** Field enforcement questions; bridge to frameworks in a5.

## Preflight
- Open a4, "Expand all", confirm the three-tier fine SVG and the enforcement diagram render.
- Have a calculator or spreadsheet ready so the ceiling exercise is real, using the learner's own (or Vantage's) turnover.
- Be clear on the SME/startup flip before you present it.

## Never-cut beats
- **The three tiers, in order.** 7%/EUR 35M (prohibited), 3%/EUR 15M (high-risk and most obligations), 1%/EUR 7.5M (wrong information). For a large firm the higher of cap-or-percentage applies.
- **The SME flip.** For SMEs and startups the fine is capped at the LOWER of the cap or the percentage - deliberately proportionate to size.
- **High-risk is enforced nationally.** Not by the AI Office. That is a common mix-up.

## Cuts if running long
- Do the ceiling computation for one tier only (the 3% high-risk tier that most in-scope firms face).
- Summarise the aggravating/mitigating factors verbally instead of ranking them.

## Quiz answers
1. **C** - Up to EUR 35M or 7% of worldwide turnover, whichever is higher, for prohibited-practice breaches (Art 99).
2. **B** - A national market surveillance authority enforces high-risk; a non-compliant high-risk system sits in the 3%/EUR 15M tier.
3. **A** - For an SME/startup the fine is the lower of the fixed cap or the percentage.

## Common questions + crisp answers
- "Is it 7% of EU revenue?" No - it is global turnover, which is what makes it a board number.
- "Who actually fines us - Brussels?" For high-risk, a national market surveillance authority; the AI Office handles GPAI.
- "Does cooperating reduce the fine?" Authorities weigh cooperation, severity, and whether harm was mitigated - so evidence of good governance helps.

## Teaching tips
- Make the ceiling real. A percentage of the learner's actual global turnover lands harder than any abstract EUR figure - do the arithmetic live.
- Keep the three tiers in strict order; leaders remember "7, 3, 1" if you say it as a ladder rather than three separate facts.
- The SME flip is a relief moment - use it to keep smaller firms engaged rather than fatalistic about the big number.
- Correct the "Brussels fines us" assumption early; national market surveillance authorities enforce high-risk, and that changes who they will actually deal with.
- Tie the number back to a6: the fine is the reason a program with one owner and real evidence is cheaper than the alternative.

## Compliance caution
