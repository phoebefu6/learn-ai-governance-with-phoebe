Why AI governance now and how the Act reaches you

AI governance used to be a values statement. Since the EU AI Act became binding law, it is a compliance obligation with fines up to 7% of global turnover - and it does not stop at Europe's border. If your AI system's output is used in the EU, your Singapore company is in scope. This session gives you the landscape: why governance is now a board topic, the three frameworks that matter, and the one test for whether the Act reaches you.

Part 0

Why this hour is worth your calendar

Three things changed at once. AI is now making or shaping real decisions - who gets hired, who gets credit - at a scale and opacity that outruns old controls. Regulators responded: the EU AI Act is the first comprehensive, binding AI law, and it is written to follow AI wherever its effects land. And a set of frameworks - NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework - grew up to turn "govern your AI" from a slogan into a runnable program. This hour puts the three together and answers the first question a Singapore leader must ask: does the EU AI Act apply to us?

Part 1 · covers the governance landscape

Law, method, proof 9 min live

The confusing part of AI governance is that several big things share the name. They are not competitors - they are three layers. One is the law you must obey; one is how to think about risk; one is how to run and prove your governance.

LiveThe one line that untangles it3 min▶

Say it this way: "NIST shows what good looks like, ISO 42001 lets you prove it, and the EU AI Act makes parts of it mandatory."

  • EU AI Act: binding law. Risk-tiered obligations, prohibited uses, provider/deployer duties, real fines. If you are in scope, this is the floor - not optional.
  • NIST AI RMF: a voluntary, US-origin risk method (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage). It gives your teams a common language for AI risk and maps neatly onto the Act and ISO.
  • ISO/IEC 42001: the first certifiable AI management-system standard. It is how you run governance operationally and show an auditor or a customer third-party-verified proof.
  • Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework: voluntary, principles-based, the responsible-AI reference for the region - useful even where the Act does not reach.
★ Try it now (any chat AI)Explain the difference between the EU AI Act, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, and ISO/IEC 42001 to a board member in plain English - which is law, which is voluntary, which is certifiable - and how a company would use all three together.
LiveWhy governance became a board topic3 min▶
  • The decisions got consequential. AI now screens job applicants, scores credit, triages services. When it is wrong or biased, the harm is real and the liability is yours.
  • The law got teeth. The EU AI Act's top fine tier is EUR 35 million or 7% of global turnover - a board-level number, larger than the GDPR's.
  • The scrutiny got public. Customers, employees, and regulators now ask "how is this AI governed?" - and "we're careful" is not an answer you can evidence.

Part 2 · covers Article 2 territorial reach

When the Act reaches Singapore 8 min live

The EU AI Act uses a market-and-output model, not a where-are-you model. Like the GDPR, it follows the effect. For a Singapore company the trigger is simple, and it catches more firms than they expect.

LiveThe output-in-the-Union trigger3 min▶

Article 2 puts you in scope if any of these is true, regardless of where your company sits:

  • You place an AI system (or a GPAI model) on the EU market or put it into service there - even through an EU reseller.
  • You are a deployer located in the EU.
  • You are a provider or deployer in a third country and the output of your AI system is used in the Union.

That third limb is the one that surprises Singapore firms. A model run entirely in Singapore, whose results are used to make decisions about people in the EU - screening EU job applicants, scoring EU loan seekers - is in scope. A purely Singapore-internal system whose output never touches the EU is not.

The pattern you already know This mirrors the GDPR's extraterritorial reach (session-mates who took the GDPR course will recognise it): the law follows the EU individual, not your headquarters. If you serve or affect people in the EU with AI, assume you are in scope until you have checked.
Self-studyGovernance is bigger than the Act2 min read▶

It would be a mistake to reduce AI governance to "comply with the EU AI Act". The Act is the sharpest legal edge, but governance is the broader discipline of making sure your AI is trustworthy, accountable, and defensible - whether or not a specific law compels it. Even a Singapore company entirely outside the Act's reach has customers, employees, and a reputation that depend on its AI behaving well, and Singapore's own Model AI Governance Framework exists precisely to guide that. So treat the Act as the forcing function that makes governance concrete and urgent, but build the program for the wider goal: an AI operation you can explain, evidence, and stand behind. That framing also future-proofs you - more AI laws are coming, and a real governance program adapts to the next one far more cheaply than a bare EU-AI-Act checklist does.

Exercise 1 of 2

Does the Act reach you? ★ 12 min · pen and paper

Run the Article 2 test on your own AI systems.

List the AI systems your company builds or uses - the CV screener, the recommender, the chatbot, the credit model.

For each, ask: do we place it on the EU market, or is its output used in the EU (about EU people)? Mark in scope / out of scope.

For the in-scope ones, note your likely role - are you the provider (you built it) or a deployer (you use someone else's)? This decides who carries the obligations.

Circle the highest-stakes in-scope system. That is where the rest of this course pays off first.

Exercise 2 of 2

Map your frameworks ★ 8 min · your next AI review

Four questions that reveal whether your governance is a program or a slide.

"Which of our AI systems are in scope of the EU AI Act, and what tier are they?" If nobody can answer, governance has not started.

"Do we use a risk method like the NIST AI RMF, or do we assess AI risk ad hoc?" A common method beats hero effort.

"Could we show a customer or regulator evidence of our governance - or just describe it?" This is the ISO 42001 question: proof vs assertion.

"Who owns AI governance, and does it connect to our data governance and our model evaluations?" Three disconnected efforts is the common failure.

The mindset The Act sets the floor; the frameworks build the house. A mature program uses all three - the law for what is mandatory, NIST for how to reason about risk, ISO to prove it - run by one owner, not three.
