Penalties and enforcement what non-compliance actually costs

Sessions 1 to 3 told you the Act reaches you, sorted your systems by risk tier, and named who carries which duty. This session puts a number on getting it wrong. The EU AI Act's fines are tiered like its risks, they are calculated on your worldwide turnover, and its top tier is larger than the GDPR's. You will also learn who actually enforces the Act - the EU AI Office for the big general-purpose models, and a national authority in each member state that is your single point of contact.

Part 0

Why the number matters to a board

A risk tier is abstract until it has a price. This session gives the Act's obligations their teeth: three fine tiers that mirror the risk tiers, a base of global turnover rather than EU-only revenue, and enforcement bodies with the power to investigate and penalise. For a board, this is the slide that turns "we should look at AI governance" into "we will fund AI governance this quarter" - because the downside is now a figure on the income statement, not a vague reputational worry.

Read this first Educational, not legal advice. Penalty figures and enforcement structures are set by the Regulation and applied by national authorities that may set their own procedures. Verify against the Act's text and get proper advice before acting.

Part 1 · covers Article 99 penalties

The three fine tiers 9 min live

The Act does not have one fine. It has a ladder, and where you land depends on which kind of rule you broke. The worst breach - doing something the Act prohibits outright - carries the heaviest fine. Breaching an ordinary obligation is the middle tier. Giving an authority wrong or misleading information is the lightest. Every ceiling is a pair: a fixed euro cap or a percentage of your global turnover.

What you didFixed cap% of worldwide turnover
Broke a prohibition (Art 5 banned practice)EUR 35 million7%
Breached other obligations (high-risk duties, Art 50 transparency)EUR 15 million3%
Gave incorrect, incomplete or misleading informationEUR 7.5 million1%
LiveHigher or lower - and always on global turnover3 min▶

Two details decide the real number, and both catch leaders off guard.

  • Higher of the two, for most firms. The fine is up to the greater of the fixed cap or the percentage. For a large company the percentage usually wins - 7% of a big global turnover dwarfs EUR 35 million.
  • Lower of the two, for SMEs and startups. The Act flips the rule for smaller firms: they face up to the lesser of the cap or the percentage, so the fine stays proportionate to their size.
  • The base is worldwide turnover. Not EU revenue, not the revenue of the product at fault - your group's total worldwide annual turnover for the preceding financial year. This is why the number gets large fast.

LiveBigger than the GDPR3 min▶
  • The GDPR's top tier. Data-protection leaders anchor on the GDPR's headline fine: up to EUR 20 million or 4% of global turnover, whichever is higher.
  • The AI Act goes higher. Its top tier - EUR 35 million or 7% - is larger on both halves. For a firm already GDPR-conscious, the message is simple: the AI Act raises the ceiling.
  • Why it lands. A board that respects the GDPR's number now has a bigger one to respect. The comparison is the fastest way to make AI governance a funded priority.
The pattern you already know If your organisation took the GDPR seriously, apply that same seriousness one notch higher. Same logic - percentage of global turnover, whichever is higher for large firms - just a taller ceiling.
Part 2 · covers Articles 64, 74, 101

Who enforces the Act 8 min live

A fine needs a body to impose it. The Act splits enforcement in two. The big general-purpose AI models are overseen centrally by the European AI Office. Everything else is enforced nationally, by a market surveillance authority in each member state - and that national authority is your single point of contact.

LiveThe AI Office and national authorities3 min▶

Two layers, each with a clear remit:

  • European AI Office (Art 64). A central body within the European Commission that supervises and enforces the rules for general-purpose AI models - the large foundation models. If you build or provide a GPAI model, the AI Office is your overseer.
  • National market surveillance authorities (Art 74). Each member state designates one. They enforce the Act nationally and act as the single point of contact for companies and the public. For a high-risk system placed in a given country, its authority is who you deal with.
  • GPAI-provider fines (Art 101). Providers of general-purpose AI models have their own dedicated penalty route - up to EUR 15 million or 3% of worldwide turnover - imposed by the Commission through the AI Office.
Real world

Vantage's exposure. Vantage's AI hiring tool is a high-risk system, not a general-purpose model - so its enforcer is a national market surveillance authority, not the AI Office. If the tool ships to its German customer without the conformity documentation and controls required of a high-risk system, Vantage sits in the middle tier: up to EUR 15 million or 3% of Vantage's worldwide turnover, whichever is higher. Ship a prohibited practice and it jumps to the 7% tier. That single German market entry is what put a real euro figure on the board's decision to fund governance now.

Know your enforcer before you ship Identify which member state's authority governs each market you enter, and treat it as the single point of contact from day one. The GPAI-vs-national split decides who you answer to - do not assume the AI Office handles your ordinary product.
Self-studyFines are a ceiling, not a starting price2 min read▶

It is easy to read "up to 7%" as "7%", but the figures in Article 99 are maximums, not tariffs. Authorities are directed to set penalties that are effective, proportionate and dissuasive, and to weigh the circumstances of each case - the nature and gravity of the breach, whether it was intentional or negligent, whether you cooperated and self-reported, whether anyone was actually harmed, and the size and market position of the company. A first, minor, self-reported slip by a cooperative SME is a very different matter from a large firm that shipped a banned practice and hid it. This is not a reason to relax - the ceiling is real and the reputational damage of any enforcement action is its own cost - but it is a reason to build a governance program you can point to. Evidence that you took reasonable care, documented your decisions, and moved quickly to fix problems is exactly what pulls a penalty down from its ceiling. Governance is not only how you avoid a fine; it is how you argue the fine down if one ever comes.

Exercise 1 of 2

Compute your ceilings ★ 12 min · pen and paper

Put your own numbers on the top two tiers. Abstract percentages become a board slide the moment they are your euros.

Write down your group's total worldwide annual turnover for the last financial year. Use the whole group, not just the EU or the product at fault.

Compute 7% of that figure - your prohibited-practice ceiling - and compare it to EUR 35 million. For most firms the percentage is higher; note which applies to you.

Compute 3% - your other-obligations ceiling, the one a non-compliant high-risk system sits in - and compare it to EUR 15 million.

If you are an SME or startup, remember the rule flips: your fine is capped at the lower of the two. Note both figures so the board sees the real exposure.

Exercise 2 of 2

Rank what authorities weigh ★ 8 min · your next AI review

Four questions that reveal whether you would argue a penalty up or down.

"Could we show we took reasonable care - documented decisions, risk assessments, controls?" Evidence of care is the single biggest lever on a fine.

"If a problem surfaced, would we self-report and cooperate, or hope no one notices?" Cooperation and self-reporting pull the number down; concealment pushes it up.

"Do we know which national authority is our single point of contact in each EU market we enter?" If nobody can name it, you are not ready to ship.

"Are any of our uses anywhere near a prohibited practice?" That is the 7% tier - the one worth checking first and hardest.

The mindset The fine ceiling is fixed by law, but where you land under it is decided by what you can evidence. Build the governance program so that if an authority ever asks, the answer is a folder of proof, not a shrug.

Three questions before you go 🎯 ◐ 90 seconds

1 · A large company breaches an outright prohibition under the EU AI Act. The top fine tier is...

Prohibited-practice breaches carry the top tier under Article 99: up to EUR 35M or 7% of total worldwide turnover, and for a large firm the higher of the two applies.

2 · Vantage ships its high-risk AI hiring tool. Who is its enforcer, and which tier applies to a non-compliant high-risk system?

A high-risk system is enforced nationally (Art 74), not by the AI Office. Breaching high-risk obligations sits in the middle tier: up to EUR 15M or 3%.

3 · For an SME or startup, the fine under the EU AI Act is capped at...

The Act flips the rule for SMEs and startups: they face up to the lesser of the cap or the percentage, keeping the fine proportionate to their size.
