Part 1 · covers the program hub

The program hub 9 min live

A program needs a centre. Put one person in it - an AI governance lead - and arrange the artifacts around them. Not a committee that meets quarterly and disperses, but a named owner with a registry of every AI system and a handful of artifacts they keep alive. This is what turns five sessions of knowledge into something that runs.

LiveOne owner, a registry, the artifacts3 min▶

A program is four things assembled around a person:

  • One owner. An AI governance lead - a named individual accountable for the whole, with a seat close to the board. Scattered ownership is the number-one failure; a single owner is the number-one fix.
  • An AI system inventory / registry. A living list of every AI system you build or use, each tagged with its risk tier (a2) and your role (a3). You cannot govern what you have not listed.
  • Defined roles. Provider or deployer duties assigned per system, so responsibility is never ambiguous.
  • The artifacts. A risk method (NIST), a management system (ISO 42001), an EU AI Act compliance mapping, model evaluations as evidence the system works, and incident-and-monitoring to catch problems early.

LiveWhy the registry comes first3 min▶
  • You cannot govern an unknown. Most organisations underestimate how many AI systems they run - shadow tools, embedded features, vendor models. The registry surfaces them.
  • It carries the tier and the role. Each entry links to session a2 (which risk tier) and a3 (provider or deployer), so the registry is where the whole course lands in one table.
  • It drives the cadence. A new system or a new feature is a trigger to review - the registry is what the review works from. No registry, no habit.
Build the registry before the policy A polished policy governing systems you have not inventoried is theatre. List every AI system first - tier and role per row - and the rest of the program has something real to attach to.
Part 2 · covers the operating-system view

One operating system, not three silos 8 min live

The most expensive mistake is treating AI governance, data governance, and model evaluation as three separate programs. They are one operating system. Governance reads from the data that feeds your models and from the evals that show the models work. Wire them together and each makes the others stronger; keep them apart and all three drift.

LiveHow the three connect3 min▶
  • Governance reads from data governance. A model is only as trustworthy as the data feeding it. Provenance, quality, and consent for that data are a data-governance job - and AI governance depends on it. If you took the data-governance course, this is where it plugs in.
  • Governance reads from evals. "The model works" is not a claim, it is an evidence question. Model evaluations - accuracy, bias, retrieval quality - produce exactly the proof a regulator or customer asks for. The RAG and evals courses build this muscle.
  • Make it a habit. Tie a review cadence to real triggers: a new AI system, a major feature, an incident. Compliance is a habit, not a project - a program that only wakes up for an audit has already failed.
Real world

Vantage stands up its program. Vantage named a head of data as its AI governance lead, built a one-page registry that listed the hiring tool as high-risk with Vantage as provider, and hung the artifacts off it: the NIST method it already used for bias, the ISO 42001 path for its German customer, an EU AI Act compliance map, the evals that measured the tool's fairness, and an incident channel. Crucially, it did not build these in isolation - the registry drew on the same data-governance catalogue that tracked the training data, and the fairness evals became the evidence its compliance map pointed to. One owner, one operating system. The board's someday-item was now a running program with a 90-day plan and a quarterly review.

Do not rebuild what you have If you already run data governance or model evals, do not stand up parallel AI-governance versions. Point your governance program at the systems you already have and connect them - one operating system is cheaper and truer than three.

Exercise 1 of 2

Score your program artifacts ★ 12 min · pen and paper

Five artifacts, a quick honest score. Where the zeros are is where your program starts.

Score each on 0 (none) / 1 (partial) / 2 (in place): a named owner, an AI system registry, a risk method (NIST), a management system (ISO 42001), and model evaluations.

Add them up. A total under 5 means the program is aspiration, not operation - and that is fine as a starting point, as long as you name it.

Circle every zero. Those are the gaps that block everything downstream - especially a missing owner or a missing registry.

Check the connections: does governance read from your data governance and your evals, or do they run as silos? Note that separately.

Exercise 2 of 2

Write a 90-day plan ★ 8 min · your next leadership meeting

Four moves that turn today's knowledge into a plan your board can fund.

Days 1-30: name the owner and build the registry. Appoint the AI governance lead and inventory every AI system with its tier and role. Nothing else can start without these two.

Days 31-60: adopt a method and map the Act. Put the NIST functions to work on your highest-risk system, and map its EU AI Act obligations.

Days 61-90: wire in proof and connections. Set the ISO 42001 path if a customer needs it, connect governance to your data governance and evals, and stand up incident monitoring.

Set the cadence. Fix a recurring review tied to new systems and features so the program stays a habit after day 90.

The mindset A 90-day plan beats a perfect policy. Name the owner, list the systems, put the method to work on the riskiest one, and set a cadence. Compliance is a habit, not a launch - the plan exists to start the habit, not to finish the work.
